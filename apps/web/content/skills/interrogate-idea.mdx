You had an idea at 11pm. You opened your AI tool, typed a prompt, and started building.

By morning, you'd shipped something. It looked good. It worked.

And then — nothing. No one cared. No traction. No revenue. Just an app that technically functions, for an audience that doesn't exist.

You've done this before. Everyone has.

The problem isn't the building. It's what came before it.

## The Gap No One Talks About

Everyone talks about execution. Ship faster. Iterate. Use AI to build in a weekend.

But no one talks about the moment _before_ you build — when you're still just holding an idea and a dream.

That's where most products die.

Not in production. Not in shipping. In the gap between "interesting idea" and "should we actually build this."

Most people don't cross that gap consciously. They just... start building. And then wonder why three months later, no one wants what they made.

## What You're Actually Missing

When you prompt AI with "build me an app for X," you're assuming X is real, people care, and the market exists.

AI doesn't challenge those assumptions. It just builds fast.

The problem isn't AI. It's that you've given AI a vague brief — and vague briefs produce vague results. Garbage in, garbage out.

You wouldn't hire a developer and say "build me something good." But that's essentially what you're doing when you prompt AI without pressure-testing the idea first.

## Interrogation Is the Discipline

Before you write a line of code, you need to know three things:

**Does a real customer have this problem?** Not "people might want this" — a specific person, with a specific pain, that you can describe in one sentence.

**Will anyone pay?** "Users" aren't the same as "customers." Free users are a vanity metric. If you can't name how this makes money, you don't have a business — you have a hobby.

**Can you actually build it?** Not "can it be built in theory" — can you ship something meaningful in 2-4 weeks? Can you name the technical unknowns that would kill it?

If you can answer all three, you're ready to build. If not, you're building blind.

That's interrogation. It's not a checklist. It's a filter — a way to catch the gaps before they become months of wasted effort.

## The Double-Diamond Foundation

Most product frameworks tell you to "validate" before building. But they don't tell you _what to validate_ or _how to know when you're done_.

Interrogation breaks validation into three dimensions. Each dimension has a gate. Pass the gate, or close the gap.

The dimensions aren't arbitrary — they're where most indie-hackers and PMs are weakest:

- **Customer Experience** — Most builders skip straight to solution. They fall in love with what they want to make, not what the customer actually needs.

- **Business** — "Build it and they will come" is a fantasy. If you can't describe who pays and why, you're not building a product — you're building a prototype.

- **Code** — "We'll figure it out" is not a plan. Technical unknowns kill projects. Surface them early.

Interrogation forces you to face each dimension. You can't paper over a gap. You either close it or you accept that you're proceeding blind.

## Why This Works

When you interrogate an idea, something shifts. The vague excitement becomes specific. The "everyone needs this" becomes "amateur bakers aged 25-45 who follow sourdough influencers."

The "it'll be free with premium features" becomes "I have no idea how this earns money."

You find the gaps. And either you close them — or you kill the idea before investing six months in something doomed.

This is the discipline AI can't provide. AI accelerates. It doesn't interrogate.

## The Skill, Not the Theory

Interrogation starts as a conversation. One question at a time. Your answers reveal the gaps. The gaps tell you what to do next.

After interrogation, you'll have a score for each dimension. If a dimension is incomplete, you know exactly what's missing. You can close it — with another skill, with research, with honest thinking — before you write a single line of code.

Or you'll realize the idea isn't ready. And that's a win too. Better to kill a bad idea on day one than after three months of building something no one wants.

## What Interrogation Is Not

It's not a way to kill ideas. It's a way to know which ideas are worth your time.

It's not a checklist you tick and ignore. It's a conversation that surfaces the gaps you were avoiding.

It's not a one-time gate. You can return to interrogation whenever new information changes the picture.

## Ready to Interrogate?

Before you build your next idea — any idea — run it through interrogation first.

You'll either get a green light you actually earned, or you'll discover why your idea isn't ready yet.

Either way, you'll save time. The only bad interrogation result is the one you ignore.
